title: "I don't know"
date: 2020-03-14

It’s a good step to learn to say “I don’t know.” Considering how opaque the mind’s inner functions remain, answering questions with “I don’t know” is a good bet. It’s always partially true and especially so if the question has to do with human behavior, or another complex system.

I remember coming out of the subway in Crown Heights, Brooklyn, and a TV reporter asked for my thoughts about an NYC education policy. I looked straight in the camera and said happily, “I don’t know.” The camera crew seemed to appreciate the candor.

“I don’t know” is useful not in that it produces the right answer, but in that the phrase resets the search for a correct answer. If we’re on the wrong track, saying “I don’t know” gets us back to neutral.

The problem with “I don’t know” though, is that the phrase can’t get you to what’s maximally useful. It can get you closer to the right answer if you were going the wrong way, but “I don’t know” can’t take you all the way. At the risk of exposing my naiveté, but with the potential upside of a reader’s smile: it’s good to have the right answer.

The right price generates the most revenue. The right feature attracts the most users. The right spouse makes a man thrive. While these statements are devastatingly obvious to me now, that wasn’t true in my self-righteous early 20s. Now, there’s a more right and wrong thing to do at every moment, and I don’t mean morally.

A younger me found "I don't know"'s broad applicability infatuating. It takes some guts to say it, to expose your ignorance. Plus, it’s a way to never be wrong. Because, outside of math, and even then I’m not sure, you do really never “know.” Still, I’d like to find myself saying “I don’t know,” less.

You can be lazy and still be right when you say “I don’t know.” And being lazy is often wrong.

The scary thing is trying to really answer questions. “What’s the highest impact action a person can reasonably take in 15 minutes to mitigate climate change?” takes a while to figure out. Physical-world questions like “How does the watch you’re wearing work?” still stump me. "Why do the materials in front of you even stay solid?" And so on. Fun game for non-physics majors.

While the questions above are simply asked, they’re difficult to answer. Even if decent answers are sometimes only one sentence long, it’s hard for me to construct a correct one without the internet’s aid. Despite the unknown’s prevalence, our minds flow forward generally unperturbed by the ignorance surrounding them (there’s probably an evolution explanation here).

Even those who strive for the right answer still can be stymied by simple questions. There’s simply too much to know. Scientists spend their lives trying to be correct. Yet a question outside their specialty is about as difficult to answer as it is to the average person. This is not to put scientists down, but to point out the fragility of the mind in the context of the complex world.

It’s not only scientists who strive for correctness, every job has the concept of correct floating above the workplace. The right length of 2x4 to cut for carpenters, the right amount of eye contact for service workers, the right choice of web framework for engineers. Everyone wants to be right.

But despite this near constant search to be correct, we remain exceedingly ignorant of the complex world in which we inhabit. Again, this may be old news to many. But when I think of how little I understand how complex tasks like going to the grocery store (a very complex place!) works, it really is amazing how confident I remain.

To this end, while “I have no idea what’s going on,” may be an exaggeration, it’s approximately correct, though the phrase falls into the same category as “I don’t know” as being broadly applicable, but too general to be useful.

What’s the point? Okay, life’s complex as all hell, why is this essay useful? The point depends on who’s reading this. If you haven’t grokked how complex the world is, and that if you change one thing, other things change too (and you don’t even know what things are going to change!), maybe this essay will help you get there. At least then you can begin to add degrees of certainty (between 0 and 100) to your opinions a bit.

Then for folks who have already figured that out, what’s the point? The point is that it’s important to have the right answer sometimes. It takes a lot of work to get the right answer, so it’s probably wise to pick your questions wisely.

If you’ve already figured all that out you probably shouldn’t be reading this essay. If you got this far and are disappointed, I’m sorry.

I also wrote it, at least partially, to inspire myself to choose questions which I am motivated to truly answer. Difficult as that is surrounded by systems which I do not understand, it’s very important to not be paralyzed, but motivated to action, and perhaps awed, by the complexity which surrounds us.
